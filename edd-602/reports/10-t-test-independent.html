T-Test for Independent Samples

Comparing the Means of Two Independent Groups

Introduction

In research, we often compare the means of two separate groups to determine whether a significant difference exists between them. For example, we may compare the performance of students using two different teaching methods.

The t-test for independent samples is used when the two groups being compared are unrelated or independent from each other.


Learning Objectives

  • Define the independent samples t-test.
  • Identify when to use the test.
  • Explain its assumptions.
  • Compute and interpret results.

Historical Background

The t-test was developed by William Sealy Gosset in 1908 under the pseudonym "Student". It was originally created to solve problems in quality control for small sample sizes in breweries.

Over time, the t-test became one of the most widely used statistical tools in educational, psychological, and social science research for comparing group means.


Definition

The independent samples t-test is a statistical test used to determine whether there is a significant difference between the means of two independent groups.


When to Use the Independent Samples T-Test

  • Two independent groups are being compared.
  • Data is continuous (interval or ratio scale).
  • Samples are randomly selected.
  • Data is approximately normally distributed.
  • Variances are equal (homogeneity of variance).

T-Test Formula

t = (X̄1 - X̄2) / sqrt( (s12 / n1) + (s22 / n2) )

Where:

  • X̄1 = mean of group 1
  • X̄2 = mean of group 2
  • s12 = variance of group 1
  • s22 = variance of group 2
  • n1 = sample size of group 1
  • n2 = sample size of group 2

Assumptions

  • The two groups are independent.
  • Data is continuous.
  • Data is approximately normally distributed.
  • Variances of both groups are equal.
  • Observations are randomly selected.

Worked Example

Research Scenario

A researcher wants to determine whether there is a significant difference in mathematics performance between students taught using Method A and Method B.

Two independent groups of students are selected for comparison.

Given Information
  • Group 1 Mean (X̄1) = 82
  • Group 2 Mean (X̄2) = 76
  • Group 1 Standard Deviation (s1) = 8
  • Group 2 Standard Deviation (s2) = 10
  • Group 1 Sample Size (n1) = 30
  • Group 2 Sample Size (n2) = 30

Solution

Step 1 - State the Hypotheses

We begin by stating the null and alternative hypotheses.

  • H0 (Null Hypothesis): There is no significant difference between the two group means (X̄1 and X̄2).
  • H1 (Alternative Hypothesis): There is a significant difference between the two group means (X̄1 and X̄2).
H0: X̄1 = X̄2
H1: X̄1 != X̄2
Step 2 - Identify the Formula

The formula for the independent samples t-test is:

t = (X̄1 - X̄2) / sqrt( (s12 / n1) + (s22 / n2) )
Step 3 - Substitute the Given Values

Given:

  • X̄1 = 82
  • X̄2 = 76
  • s1 = 8
  • s2 = 10
  • n1 = 30
  • n2 = 30

Substitute into the formula:

t = (82 - 76) / sqrt( (82 / 30) + (102 / 30) )

Step 4 - Compute the Values

Compute numerator:

82 - 76 = 6

Compute denominator:

82 = 64
102 = 100

64 / 30 = 2.13 (approx)
100 / 30 = 3.33 (approx)

2.13 + 3.33 = 5.46
sqrt(5.46) = 2.34 (approx)

Now compute t:

t = 6 / 2.34

t = 2.56 (approx)

Step 5 - Decision Rule

At a = 0.05 (two-tailed test), degrees of freedom:

df = n1 + n2 - 2 = 30 + 30 - 2 = 58

Critical value (approx):

t critical = ±2.00 (approx)

Decision rule:

  • If |t computed| > t critical, reject H0
  • If |t computed| = t critical, fail to reject H0
Step 6 - Decision
t = 2.56

Since 2.56 is greater than 2.00, we reject the null hypothesis.

Step 7 - Interpretation

There is a significant difference in mathematics performance between students taught using Method A and Method B.

This suggests that the teaching method has a statistically significant effect on student performance.


Applications

  • Comparing teaching methods
  • Gender-based performance studies
  • Experimental research designs
  • Business training evaluations

Summary

The independent samples t-test is used to compare the means of two unrelated groups. In this example, the computed t-value (2.56) exceeded the critical value (±2.00), leading to rejection of the null hypothesis and concluding that a significant difference exists between the two groups.
