Level of Variable Measurement (Nominal, Ordinal, Interval/Ratio)

1. Taro Yamane's Formula

Not a test (Sampling formula)

Used to determine sample size from a finite population. It helps researchers estimate the minimum number of respondents needed for surveys while ensuring representativeness and reducing sampling error in quantitative research designs.

Population is finite and known; simple random sampling assumed.

N/A

2. Cochran Formula

Not a test (Sampling formula)

A method for calculating sample size for large or infinite populations. It ensures accurate survey estimates by incorporating confidence level, variability, and margin of error in quantitative research studies and sampling design.

Large population assumed; normal approximation and random sampling required.

N/A

3. Fisher's Formula

Not a test (Sampling formula)

Used to estimate sample size based on expected proportion and allowable error. It is commonly applied in survey research to ensure statistically valid results and reliable representation of population characteristics.

Prior proportion estimate available; random sampling assumed.

N/A

4. Krejcie & Morgan Formula

Not a test (Sampling formula)

Provides a standardized table and formula for determining sample size based on population size. It is widely used in educational and social research for quick, practical, and reliable sampling decisions.

Known population size; random sampling and fixed confidence level assumed.

N/A

5. Jacob Cohen's Formula

Not a test (Power analysis)

Determines required sample size using effect size, significance level, and statistical power. Ensures studies have enough power to detect meaningful effects in hypothesis testing and experimental designs.

Effect size estimated; correct model specification and normality assumed.

N/A

6. Samuel B. Green Formula

Not a test (Regression guideline)

Provides minimum sample size rules in regression to ensure stable coefficients, reduce overfitting, and improve reliability of multivariate predictive models in research.

Linear relationships assumed; adequate cases per predictor required.

N/A

7. G*Power Formula

Not a test (Power analysis tool)

A software-based method for determining sample size, statistical power, and effect size across many statistical tests. It helps researchers design studies with sufficient power before data collection begins.

Correct test selection and accurate parameter input assumed.

N/A

8. z test for Comparison of Sample and Population Means

Parametric

Used to determine whether a significant difference exists between a sample mean and a known population mean. It is applied when population variance is known and sample size is large in quantitative studies.

Normal distribution; known population variance; independent observations assumed.

Interval/Ratio

9. z test for Comparison of Proportions

Parametric

Used to compare sample and population proportions or two proportions. It determines whether differences are statistically significant using normal approximation of binomial distribution in large sample studies.

Large sample size; normal approximation valid; independent observations assumed.

Nominal

10. t-test for Independent Samples

Parametric

Compares means of two independent groups to determine if a statistically significant difference exists. Commonly used in experimental and comparative research designs involving separate groups.

Normal distribution; equal variances; independent groups assumed.

Interval/Ratio

11. t-test for Dependent Samples

Parametric

Also called paired t-test, it compares means from the same group measured at two different times or conditions. It evaluates changes or effects within subjects in repeated-measures research designs.

Paired observations; normal distribution of differences assumed.

Interval/Ratio

12. One-way Analysis of Variance

Parametric

Used to compare means of three or more independent groups to determine if at least one group differs significantly. Widely used in experimental and comparative quantitative research designs.

Normality; homogeneity of variance; inde-pendent samples assumed.

Interval/Ratio

13. Pearson r

Parametric

Measures the strength and direction of linear relationship between two continuous variables. It is widely used in correlation studies to determine how strongly variables move together in a linear pattern and association.

Linear relationship; normality; homoscedasticity assumed.

Interval/Ratio

14. Linear Regression Analysis

Parametric

Examines predictive relationships between independent and dependent variables. It estimates how changes in predictors influence outcomes in continuous data for forecasting, explanation, and modeling purposes in research.

Linearity; independence; normality of residuals assumed.

Interval/Ratio

15. Path Analysis

Parametric

Extends regression analysis to examine direct and indirect relationships among multiple variables. It is used to test causal models and complex relationships in theoretical research frameworks.

Linearity; causal ordering specified; reliable measurement assumed.

Interval/Ratio

16. Mann-Whitney U statistic

Non-Parametric

Tests differences between two independent groups using ranked data. It is the non-parametric alternative to the independent t-test and is used when data are not normally distributed.

Independent groups; ordinal or non-normal data assumed.

Ordinal

17. Wilcoxon Signed Ranks Test

Non-Parametric

Compares two related samples using ranked differences. It is the non-parametric alternative to the paired t-test and is used when data violate normality assumptions in repeated measurements.

Paired observations; symmetric distribution of differences assumed.

Ordinal

18. Kruskal Wallis H statistic

Non-Parametric

Compares three or more independent groups using ranked data. It is the non-parametric alternative to one-way ANOVA and is used when normality assumptions are violated in research data.

Independent groups; ordinal data; similar distribution shapes assumed.

Ordinal

19. Friedman Test

Non-Parametric

Tests differences among three or more related groups using ranks. It is used in repeated-measures designs when data do not meet ANOVA assumptions of normality and homogeneity.

Related samples; ordinal data; consistent measurement conditions assumed.

Ordinal

20. Spearman rho

Non-Parametric

Measures rank-based correlation between two variables. It assesses monotonic relationships when data are ordinal or not normally distributed, serving as a non-parametric alternative to Pearson correlation.

Monotonic relationship; ordinal or ranked data assumed.

Ordinal

21. Repeated Measure ANOVA

Parametric

Used to compare means of three or more related groups measured at different times or conditions. It evaluates within-subject differences and is commonly applied in experimental and longitudinal research designs.

Normality of differences; sphericity assumed; related measurements required.

Interval/Ratio

22. Chi-Square Goodness
of Fit Test

Non-Parametric

Tests whether observed frequencies differ significantly from expected frequencies in a single categorical variable. It is commonly used to determine how well data fit a theoretical or expected distribution.

Random sampling; expected frequencies sufficiently large; independent observations assumed.

Nominal

23. Chi-Square Test of Indepen-dence

Non-Parametric

Determines whether there is a significant association between two categorical variables. It is widely used in survey research to analyze relationships between demographic or categorical characteristics.

Independent observations; expected frequencies adequate; random sampling assumed.

Nominal

24. Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA)

Parametric

Compares group means while controlling for the effect of one or more continuous covariates. It improves accuracy by adjusting pre-existing differences among groups in experimental and quasi-experimental designs.

Normality; linear relationship between covariate and dependent variable; homogeneity of regression slopes.

Interval/Ratio

25. Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test

Non-Parametric

Tests whether a sample distribution differs from a reference distribution, such as normal distribution. It is commonly used to assess normality in statistical data before applying parametric tests.

Continuous data; independent observations; correct reference distribution specified.

Interval/Ratio

26. Gamma
Test

Non-Parametric

Measures the strength and direction of association between two ordinal variables. It is based on concordant and discordant pairs and is used when data are ranked or ordered.

Ordinal variables; monotonic relationship assumed; no tied ranks requirement.

Ordinal

27. Biserial Correlation

Parametric

Measures relationship between a continuous variable and a naturally dichotomous variable assumed to be underlying continuous. It is used in psychometrics and educational measurement research.

One variable continuous; dichotomy assumed to represent underlying normal distribution.

Interval/Ratio (continuous) + artificially dichotomous grouping

28. Point
Biserial Correlation

Parametric

Measures relationship between a continuous variable and a true dichotomous variable. It is commonly used in test item analysis and studies involving binary outcomes and continuous scores.

One variable continuous; dichotomous variable naturally occurring; normality assumed.

Ordinal/
Dichotomized + Interval/Ratio

29. Tetrachoric Correlation

Parametric

Estimates correlation between two variables that are both artificially dichotomized from continuous distributions. It is widely used in psychological and educational measurement for latent trait analysis, assessment modeling, and psychometric scaling applications.

Both variables assumed continuous normal distribution; dichotomization considered artificial.

Dichotomous (derived from continuous)

30. McNemar Test

Non-Parametric

Used to analyze paired nominal data in a 2x2 contingency table. It tests changes in responses before and after an intervention or between matched pairs in experimental studies.

Paired observations; dichotomous outcome; independence of pairs assumed.

Nominal

31. Phi Correlation

Non-Parametric

Measures strength of association between two binary (dichotomous) variables. It is equivalent to Pearson correlation for 2x2 categorical data and is used in contingency table analysis.

Binary variables; independent observations assumed.

Nominal

32. Contingency Coefficient

Non-Parametric

Measures the strength of association between categorical variables in larger contingency tables. It provides an indication of relationship strength but does not indicate direction of association.

Categorical variables; adequate sample size; independence of observations assumed.

Nominal

33. MANOVA

Parametric

Multivariate analysis of variance used to compare group differences across multiple dependent variables simultaneously. It reduces Type I error and is widely used in complex experimental research designs involving multiple outcomes and factors for analysis.

Multivariate normality; homogeneity of covariance matrices; independent observations assumed.

Interval/Ratio

34. MANCOVA

Parametric

Extends MANOVA by comparing group differences across multiple dependent variables while controlling for one or more covariates. It improves accuracy by adjusting for pre-existing differences among groups in experimental and quasi-experimental research designs.

Multivariate normality; homogeneity of covariance; linear relationship between covariate and dependent variables assumed.

Interval/Ratio

35. Skewness

Not a test (Descriptive statistic)

Measures the degree of asymmetry in a data distribution. It indicates whether values are concentrated more on the left or right side of the mean, helping assess normality in statistical data analysis.

Continuous quantitative data assumed for distributional interpretation.

Interval/Ratio

36. Kurtosis

Not a test (Descriptive statistic)

Describes the peakedness or flatness of a distribution compared to a normal curve. It helps determine whether data are heavy-tailed or light-tailed, supporting assessment of normality in statistical analysis.

Continuous quantitative data assumed for distributional interpretation.

Interval/Ratio

37. Lambda

Non-Parametric

Measures proportional reduction in error when predicting one nominal variable based on another. It is used to assess association between categorical variables in contingency table analysis.

Nominal variables; directional prediction assumed; categorical data required.

Nominal

38. Somers’ d

Non-Parametric

Measures association between ordinal variables, considering directionality of dependent and independent variables. It improves interpretation of ranked relationships in contingency table analysis and ordinal data comparisons.

Ordinal variables; monotonic relationship assumed; paired ranking required.

Ordinal

39. Kendall’s tau-b

Non-Parametric

Measures ordinal association between two variables, adjusting for ties. It evaluates strength and direction of relationship, especially useful when data contain many tied ranks.

Ordinal data; monotonic relationship assumed; ties expected and adjusted.

Ordinal

40. Kendall’s tau-c

Non-Parametric

Measures association between ordinal variables in rectangular contingency tables. It is suitable for large tables where ranks are unevenly distributed, providing a measure of ordinal correlation strength.

Ordinal variables; monotonic relationship assumed; no strict tie adjustment like tau-b.

Ordinal

41. Kappa

Non-Parametric

Measures agreement between two raters or classifiers beyond chance. It is commonly used in reliability studies, especially in categorical data coding and diagnostic agreement assessments.

Independent raters; categorical data; same subjects rated by both raters assumed.

Nominal

42. Eta

Parametric

Measures the strength of association between a categorical independent variable and a continuous dependent variable. It is commonly used in ANOVA to estimate effect size.

Independent groups; normal distribution within groups assumed.

Nominal (IV) + Interval/Ratio (DV)

43. Crosstabs

Not a test (Descriptive tool)

A tabular method used to display the relationship between two or more categorical variables. It helps summarize frequency distributions and identify patterns or associations in survey and categorical data analysis.

Categorical variables; independent observations assumed.

Nominal
