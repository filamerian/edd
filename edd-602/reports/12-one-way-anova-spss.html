Lesson 12

One-Way ANOVA (Full Computation)

SPSS-Style Step-by-Step Statistical Analysis

Introduction

This lesson demonstrates the full computation of One-Way ANOVA using a structured SPSS-style approach. The goal is to determine whether there are significant differences among three teaching methods.


Research Problem

A researcher wants to determine whether three teaching methods produce significantly different student performance outcomes.


Given Data

Group Scores (Mean) n
Method A 80 20
Method B 75 20
Method C 85 20

Step 1 - Compute Grand Mean

GM = (80 + 75 + 85) / 3 = 80

Step 2 - Compute Between-Group Variation (SSB)

Formula:

SSB = n Σ (X̄i - GM)2

Computation:

  • (80 - 80)2 = 0
  • (75 - 80)2 = 25
  • (85 - 80)2 = 25
SSB = 20(0 + 25 + 25) = 1000

Step 3 - Degrees of Freedom

  • dfbetween = k - 1 = 3 - 1 = 2
  • dfwithin = N - k = 60 - 3 = 57

Step 4 - Mean Square Between

MSB = SSB / df = 1000 / 2 = 500

Step 5 - Next Step Preview

Next we will compute: SSW (Within-Group Variation), MSW, and the final F-ratio.

Step 6 - Compute Within-Group Variation (SSW)

In a full ANOVA computation, SSW represents the variation inside each group. For instructional purposes, we use a structured dataset approach.

Assume computed within-group sum of squares:

SSW = 2850

Step 7 - Compute Mean Square Within (MSW)

MSW = SSW / dfwithin = 2850 / 57 = 50

Step 8 - Compute F-Ratio

F = MSB / MSW = 500 / 50 = 10.00

Step 9 - ANOVA Summary Table (SPSS Style)

Source of Variation SS df MS F
Between Groups 1000 2 500 10.00
Within Groups 2850 57 50
Total 3850 59

Step 10 - Decision Rule

At a = 0.05, with dfbetween = 2 and dfwithin = 57:

F critical = ≈3.16
Since 10.00 > 3.16, reject H0

Step 11 - Interpretation

There is a statistically significant difference among the three teaching methods. This means that at least one group mean is different from the others.

The results suggest that the teaching method has a significant effect on student performance.


Step 12 - Conclusion

Based on the ANOVA results (F = 10.00, p < 0.05), we reject the null hypothesis. Therefore, there is sufficient evidence that the group means are significantly different.
