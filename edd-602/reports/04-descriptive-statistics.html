EdD 602 - Advanced Educational Statistics

Descriptive Statistics

Learning Objectives

At the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Define descriptive statistics.
  • Explain the importance of descriptive statistics in research.
  • Differentiate measures of central tendency and measures of variability.
  • Identify the appropriate descriptive statistics for different types of data.
  • Interpret descriptive statistical results presented in research reports.

Introduction

Before conducting hypothesis testing or advanced statistical analyses, researchers first need to understand the characteristics of their data. This process is known as descriptive statistics.

Descriptive statistics summarize, organize, and present data in a meaningful manner. Instead of examining hundreds or thousands of individual observations, researchers can describe an entire dataset using only a few statistical values.

Remember: Descriptive statistics describe the data, while inferential statistics draw conclusions about a population.

What are Descriptive Statistics?

Descriptive statistics are statistical methods used to summarize, organize, and present data in a meaningful and understandable form.

These statistics help researchers answer questions such as:

  • What is the average score of the students?
  • How spread out are the scores?
  • Which score occurs most frequently?
  • Are the data generally high or low?
Descriptive statistics summarize what the data show, but they do not determine whether differences or relationships are statistically significant.

Why are Descriptive Statistics Important?

Importance Explanation
Organizes Data Converts large amounts of raw data into meaningful summaries.
Simplifies Interpretation Makes research findings easier to understand.
Detects Patterns Helps identify trends, clusters, and unusual observations.
Supports Decision-Making Provides information before performing inferential statistical tests.
Improves Research Reporting Summarizes findings clearly using tables, charts, and statistical measures.

Major Categories of Descriptive Statistics

Descriptive statistics are generally grouped into four major categories.

Category Purpose Examples
Measures of Frequency Count how often observations occur. Frequency, Percentage
Measures of Central Tendency Identify the center of the data. Mean, Median, Mode
Measures of Variability Measure how spread out the data are. Range, Variance, Standard Deviation
Measures of Position Show the relative location of observations. Quartiles, Percentiles, Deciles

1. Measures of Frequency

Measures of frequency indicate how many times a value or category appears in a dataset.

They are commonly used with nominal and ordinal data and are often presented in frequency distribution tables.

Example
Program Frequency
BSIT 45
BSED 38
BSBA 42

2. Measures of Central Tendency

Measures of central tendency identify the center or typical value of a dataset. They answer the question: "What is the typical or average value?"

Measure Description Best Used For
Mean Arithmetic average of all observations. Interval and ratio data.
Median Middle value after arranging data. Ordinal data or skewed distributions.
Mode Most frequently occurring value. Nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio data.
In the next lessons, you will learn how to compute and interpret the mean, median, and mode in greater detail.

3. Measures of Variability

Measures of variability describe how widely the data are spread around the center. While measures of central tendency identify the typical value, measures of variability indicate whether the observations are closely grouped or widely scattered.

Two groups may have the same average but very different levels of variation.

Measure Description Purpose
Range Difference between the highest and lowest values. Provides the simplest measure of spread.
Variance Average squared deviation from the mean. Measures overall variability of the data.
Standard Deviation Square root of the variance. Most commonly reported measure of dispersion.
Example

Class A Scores: 78, 79, 80, 81, 82

Class B Scores: 60, 70, 80, 90, 100

Both classes have the same mean of 80, but Class B has a much larger spread of scores. Therefore, Class B has a higher variance and standard deviation.

4. Measures of Position

Measures of position describe the location of a value relative to the rest of the dataset. They divide the data into equal parts to help researchers understand where a particular observation falls.

Measure Description
Quartiles Divide the data into four equal parts.
Deciles Divide the data into ten equal parts.
Percentiles Divide the data into one hundred equal parts.
Example

A student whose test score is at the 90th percentile performed better than approximately 90% of the students who took the same examination.

Choosing the Appropriate Descriptive Statistic

The choice of descriptive statistic depends on the level of measurement of the data.

Level of Measurement Recommended Descriptive Statistics
Nominal Frequency, Percentage, Mode
Ordinal Frequency, Percentage, Median, Mode
Interval Mean, Standard Deviation, Variance, Range
Ratio Mean, Standard Deviation, Variance, Range, Percentiles

Descriptive Statistics versus Inferential Statistics

Descriptive Statistics Inferential Statistics
Summarize existing data. Draw conclusions about a population.
Describe the characteristics of a dataset. Test hypotheses and make predictions.
No generalization beyond the data collected. Generalizes sample results to a population.
Examples: Mean, Median, Standard Deviation. Examples: t-Test, ANOVA, Pearson Correlation.

Common Mistakes

  • Using the mean for highly skewed data when the median is more appropriate.
  • Reporting averages without describing the variability of the data.
  • Using descriptive statistics alone to make conclusions about a population.
  • Choosing descriptive statistics that do not match the level of measurement.
  • Interpreting descriptive statistics as evidence of cause-and-effect relationships.

Lesson Summary

  • Descriptive statistics summarize and organize data.
  • The four major categories are frequency, central tendency, variability, and position.
  • Measures of central tendency identify the typical value.
  • Measures of variability describe how spread out the data are.
  • Measures of position indicate the relative location of observations.
  • The choice of descriptive statistic depends on the level of measurement.

Self-Check Questions

  1. What is the purpose of descriptive statistics?
  2. Differentiate measures of central tendency from measures of variability.
  3. When is the median preferred over the mean?
  4. What does a large standard deviation indicate?
  5. Which descriptive statistics would you report for nominal data?
