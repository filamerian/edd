Question 1

Create a Frequency Distribution for Mathematical Ability

What is this question asking?

This question asks you to organize the Mathematical Ability scores into a frequency table. Instead of looking at a long list of scores, SPSS groups the scores together so you can easily see how many students fall within each score range.

The professor demonstrated creating score intervals first using Visual Binning before generating the frequency table. This is the procedure followed in this reviewer.

Why is this important?

A frequency distribution allows you to quickly understand how the scores are distributed. It answers questions such as:

  • Which score range has the most students?
  • Which score range has the fewest students?
  • Are most students getting high or low scores?
  • How are the scores spread across the class?

SPSS Procedure

Step 1

Click
Transform -> Visual Binning
Step 2

Select Mathematical Ability from the list of variables. Click the arrow button to move it into the Variables to Bin box.
Step 3

Click Continue. SPSS displays the minimum and maximum values of the scores. The professor computed:
  • Range = Highest Score - Lowest Score
  • Class Width = Range divided by the desired number of class intervals
During the lecture, five class intervals were used.
Step 4

Create the cut points (bins). Give the new variable a name such as
Ability2
This new variable will contain the grouped score intervals.
Step 5

Click
OK
SPSS creates the new grouped variable.
Step 6

Now create the frequency table. Click
Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies
Step 7

Move the new variable
Ability2
to the Variable(s) box. Click
OK

What Will SPSS Display?

SPSS will produce a Frequency Table. The table usually contains the following columns:

Column Meaning
Frequency Number of students in each score interval.
Percent Percentage of all students in each score interval.
Valid Percent Percentage after excluding missing data.
Cumulative Percent Running total percentage from the first interval to the last.

How to Interpret the Results

Frequency

This tells you how many students belong to a particular score interval.

Example:

28 - 35 = Frequency of 12
This means that 12 students obtained scores between 28 and 35.

Percent

This tells you what percentage of the entire class belongs to that score interval.

Example:

24%
This means that 24 percent of all students scored within that range.

Cumulative Percent

This shows the running total of percentages from the lowest score interval up to the current interval. The last row should normally reach 100%.

Professor's Notes
  • Create the score intervals first using Visual Binning.
  • Generate the frequency table using the newly created variable instead of the original Mathematical Ability scores.
  • The frequency table becomes easier to read because the scores are grouped into intervals instead of listing every individual score.
Common Mistakes
  • Using the original Mathematical Ability variable instead of the binned variable.
  • Creating too many or too few class intervals.
  • Forgetting to click OK after creating the new binned variable.
  • Interpreting Percent and Frequency as the same value.

Question 2

Compute the Mean, Median, and Mode of English Proficiency

What is this question asking?

This question asks you to compute the three measures of central tendency of the English Proficiency scores. These measures help describe the typical or average performance of the students.

Instead of looking at every individual score, these statistics summarize the entire set of data into values that are easy to understand.

Why is this important?

Measures of central tendency help answer questions such as:

  • What is the average score of the class?
  • What is the middle score of the class?
  • Which score appears most often?

These values provide a quick summary of the students' overall performance.

SPSS Procedure

Step 1

Click
Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies
Step 2

Select
English Proficiency
Move it to the Variable(s) box.
Step 3

Click
Statistics
A new window will appear.
Step 4

Place a check mark on the following:
  • Mean
  • Median
  • Mode
Click
Continue
Step 5

Click
OK
SPSS will generate the descriptive statistics.

What Will SPSS Display?

SPSS will display a table containing the selected descriptive statistics. For this question, focus on the following values:

Statistic Description
Mean The average score of all students.
Median The middle score when all scores are arranged from lowest to highest.
Mode The score that appears most frequently.

How to Interpret the Results

1. Mean

The Mean is the average score. It is calculated by adding all the scores together and dividing by the total number of students.

Example:

Mean = 82.45

Interpretation:

The average English Proficiency score of the students is 82.45.

2. Median

The Median is the middle value after arranging all scores from the lowest to the highest.

Half of the students scored below the median, while the other half scored above it.

Example:

Median = 83.00

Interpretation:

The middle score of the class is 83. Fifty percent of the students scored below 83, while the other fifty percent scored above 83.

3. Mode

The Mode is the score that occurs most often.

If one score appears more frequently than all the others, that score is the mode.

Example:

Mode = 85

Interpretation:

The score of 85 is the most common English Proficiency score among the students.

Sometimes SPSS displays a note stating that there are multiple modes. This means two or more scores occurred with the same highest frequency. SPSS usually reports only the smallest of those values.

How to Describe the Results in Words

A simple interpretation may be written as:

The English Proficiency scores have a mean of 82.45, a median of 83.00, and a mode of 85. This indicates that the average performance of the students is around 82 to 83, while the most frequently obtained score is 85.
Professor's Notes
  • The professor obtained the Mean, Median, and Mode from a single SPSS output.
  • There is no need to run separate analyses for each statistic.
  • Simply select all three statistics in the Statistics window before clicking OK.
Common Mistakes
  • Confusing the Mean with the Median.
  • Thinking that the Mode is always the highest score. It is actually the score that appears most often.
  • Forgetting to click the Statistics button before clicking OK.
  • Running separate analyses for Mean, Median, and Mode when they can all be obtained from one SPSS output.

Question 3

Compute the Standard Deviation, Variance, and Range of Mathematical Ability

What is this question asking?

This question asks you to measure how spread out the Mathematical Ability scores are. While the mean tells us the average score, these three statistics tell us whether the students' scores are close together or widely spread apart.

In other words, these statistics describe the variation or consistency of the students' performance.

Why is this important?

Knowing only the average score is not enough. Two classes may have the same average, but one class may have very similar scores while another has scores that are very different.

The Standard Deviation, Variance, and Range help us understand how consistent the students' scores are.

SPSS Procedure

Step 1

Click
Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies
Step 2

Select
Mathematical Ability
Move it to the Variable(s) box.
Step 3

Click
Statistics
A new window will appear.
Step 4

Place a check mark on the following statistics:
  • Standard Deviation
  • Variance
  • Range
Click
Continue
Step 5

Click
OK
SPSS will calculate the requested statistics.

What Will SPSS Display?

SPSS will display a Descriptive Statistics table containing the selected values. For this question, focus on:

Statistic Description
Standard Deviation Shows how close or far the scores are from the average.
Variance Measures the amount of variation in the scores.
Range The difference between the highest and lowest scores.

How to Interpret the Results

1. Standard Deviation (SD)

The Standard Deviation tells you how spread out the scores are around the mean.

A small Standard Deviation means that most students obtained scores that are close to the average.

A large Standard Deviation means that the students' scores vary greatly. Some students scored much higher or much lower than the average.

Example:

Standard Deviation = 4.82

Interpretation:

The Mathematical Ability scores do not differ greatly from the average. Most students scored within a few points of the mean.

2. Variance

The Variance also measures how spread out the scores are. It is closely related to the Standard Deviation because it is the square of the Standard Deviation.

You normally do not interpret the Variance by itself. It is mainly used in statistical computations. However, a larger variance still means greater variation among the scores.

Example:

Variance = 23.23

Interpretation:

The Mathematical Ability scores show some variation among the students. The larger the variance, the more spread out the scores are.

3. Range

The Range is the difference between the highest score and the lowest score.

Formula:

Range = Highest Score - Lowest Score

Example:

Highest Score = 49 Lowest Score = 11 Range = 49 - 11 = 38

Interpretation:

The difference between the highest and lowest Mathematical Ability score is 38 points.

How to Describe the Results in Words

The Mathematical Ability scores have a Standard Deviation of 4.82, a Variance of 23.23, and a Range of 38. These results indicate the amount of variation among the students' scores.
Professor's Notes
  • The professor obtained the Standard Deviation, Variance, and Range from the same Statistics window used for the Mean, Median, and Mode.
  • You do not need to perform another SPSS procedure. Simply check the additional statistics before clicking OK.
  • When comparing groups later, a lower Standard Deviation indicates that the group's scores are more consistent.
Common Mistakes
  • Confusing Standard Deviation with Variance.
  • Thinking that a larger Standard Deviation means better performance. It only means the scores are more spread out.
  • Computing the Range incorrectly. Always subtract the lowest score from the highest score.
  • Running a separate SPSS analysis when all three statistics can be obtained from the same Frequencies output.

Question 4

Compute the Skewness and Kurtosis of English Proficiency

What is this question asking?

This question asks you to determine the shape of the distribution of the English Proficiency scores. Specifically, it examines whether the scores are evenly distributed or whether they tend to cluster toward the lower or higher scores.

SPSS computes two statistics for this purpose:

  • Skewness - measures the symmetry of the distribution.
  • Kurtosis - measures the shape or peakedness of the distribution.

Why is this important?

Many statistical tests assume that the data are normally distributed. Skewness and Kurtosis help you determine whether the distribution is approximately normal or noticeably different from a normal distribution.

These statistics are often examined before performing parametric tests such as the t-test, ANOVA, and Pearson Correlation.

SPSS Procedure

Step 1

Click
Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies
Step 2

Select
English Proficiency
Move it to the Variable(s) box.
Step 3

Click
Statistics
Step 4

Place a check mark on:
  • Skewness
  • Kurtosis
Click
Continue
Step 5

Click
OK
SPSS will calculate the Skewness and Kurtosis values.

What Will SPSS Display?

The output table will contain the values for:

Statistic Description
Skewness Measures whether the scores are distributed evenly or are concentrated toward one side.
Kurtosis Measures whether the distribution is flatter or more peaked than a normal distribution.

How to Interpret Skewness

Skewness tells you whether the distribution is balanced or tilted toward one side.

Skewness Value Interpretation
Approximately 0 The distribution is approximately symmetrical or normal.
Positive (+) The distribution is skewed to the right. More students obtained lower scores, while only a few obtained very high scores.
Negative (-) The distribution is skewed to the left. More students obtained higher scores, while only a few obtained very low scores.

Simple Illustration
Negative Skew ************ *********** ********* ****** *** Most students have HIGH scores. -------------------------------------------- Normal Distribution **** ******** ************ ******** **** Scores are balanced. -------------------------------------------- Positive Skew *** ****** ********* ************ ************** Most students have LOW scores.

How to Interpret Kurtosis

Kurtosis describes the overall shape of the distribution. It tells you whether the scores are concentrated near the center or spread out.

Kurtosis Interpretation
Approximately 0 The distribution is close to normal.
Positive (+) The distribution is more peaked. Many scores are concentrated near the center.
Negative (-) The distribution is flatter. The scores are more spread out.

Simple Illustration
Negative Kurtosis ************** ************** ************** Flatter distribution -------------------------------------------- Normal ****** ************ ****** Normal shape -------------------------------------------- Positive Kurtosis *** ************** *** More peaked distribution

How to Describe the Results in Words

Example 1

Skewness = 0.18 Interpretation: The English Proficiency scores are approximately symmetrical, indicating that the distribution is close to normal.

Example 2

Skewness = -0.92 Interpretation: The distribution is negatively skewed. Most students obtained relatively high scores, while only a few students obtained low scores.

Example 3

Kurtosis = 0.35 Interpretation: The distribution is close to a normal distribution with only a slight tendency to be more peaked.
Professor's Notes
  • The professor obtained Skewness and Kurtosis from the same Statistics window used for the Mean, Median, Mode, Standard Deviation, Variance, and Range.
  • You do not need to perform another SPSS procedure. Simply check the additional statistics before clicking OK.
  • The professor explained that these values help determine whether the data are approximately normally distributed before performing parametric statistical tests.
Common Mistakes
  • Confusing Skewness with Kurtosis.
  • Thinking that positive or negative values automatically mean the data are incorrect.
  • Interpreting Skewness without considering the overall distribution of the data.
  • Running a separate SPSS analysis when these statistics can be obtained from the same Frequencies output.

Question 5

Compute the Frequency Distribution of Sex and Grade Level

What is this question asking?

This question asks you to determine how many students belong to each category of Sex and Grade Level. Unlike Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency, these variables are categorical, meaning they represent groups rather than numerical scores.

A frequency distribution counts the number of students in each category and shows the percentage that each category represents.

Why is this important?

Before analyzing data, it is important to know how the respondents are distributed. For example:

  • How many students are male?
  • How many students are female?
  • How many students belong to Grade 7, Grade 8, Grade 9, and Grade 10?

This information describes the profile of the respondents and is usually presented at the beginning of a research study.

SPSS Procedure

Step 1

Click
Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Frequencies
Step 2

Select the following variables:
  • Sex
  • Grade
Move both variables into the Variable(s) box.
Step 3

There is no need to click the Statistics button because this question only requires the frequency distribution of the categories.
Step 4

Click
OK
SPSS will generate separate frequency tables for Sex and Grade Level.

What Will SPSS Display?

SPSS will produce two frequency tables.

  • Frequency Distribution of Sex
  • Frequency Distribution of Grade Level

Each table contains the following information:

Column Description
Frequency The number of students in each category.
Percent The percentage of students in each category.
Valid Percent The percentage after excluding missing data.
Cumulative Percent The running total percentage.

How to Interpret the Results

Frequency

The Frequency column tells you how many students belong to each category.

Example:

Male = 28 Female = 22

Interpretation:

There are 28 male students and 22 female students in the study.

Percent

The Percent column shows what percentage of the total respondents belongs to each category.

Example:

Male = 56% Female = 44%

Interpretation:

Fifty-six percent of the respondents are male, while forty-four percent are female.

Grade Level

The Grade Level table is interpreted in exactly the same way. Simply report the number and percentage of students in each grade level.

Example:

Grade 7 = 15 students Grade 8 = 12 students Grade 9 = 11 students Grade 10 = 12 students

Interpretation:

The study consists of students from four grade levels, with Grade 7 having the largest number of respondents.

How to Describe the Results in Words

The frequency distribution shows that there are 28 male students (56%) and 22 female students (44%). The respondents came from four grade levels, with Grade 7 having the highest number of students.
Professor's Notes
  • This procedure is used only for categorical variables such as Sex and Grade Level.
  • The professor simply generated the frequency tables without computing additional descriptive statistics.
  • These tables describe the characteristics of the respondents.
Common Mistakes
  • Trying to compute the Mean or Standard Deviation for Sex or Grade Level.
  • Using Mathematical Ability instead of Sex or Grade Level.
  • Confusing Frequency with Percent.
  • Reporting only the Frequency without mentioning the corresponding Percent.

Question 6

Compute the Frequency Distribution of Sex According to Grade Level

What is this question asking?

This question asks you to determine how the students are distributed according to both Sex and Grade Level at the same time.

Instead of creating two separate frequency tables, this question combines the two variables into a single table called a Crosstab or Cross-tabulation Table.

The Crosstab allows you to compare two categorical variables and observe how they are related.

Why is this important?

A Crosstab helps answer questions such as:

  • How many Grade 7 students are male?
  • How many Grade 7 students are female?
  • Which grade level has the most male students?
  • Which grade level has the most female students?
  • Is one sex more common in a particular grade level?

This provides a clearer picture of how the respondents are distributed across different categories.

SPSS Procedure

Step 1

Click
Analyze -> Descriptive Statistics -> Crosstabs
Step 2

Select
Sex
Move it to the Row(s) box.
Step 3

Select
Grade
Move it to the Column(s) box.
Step 4

Leave the default settings. Click
OK

What Will SPSS Display?

SPSS will generate a Crosstabulation Table. The table displays the number of students for every combination of Sex and Grade Level.

A sample Crosstab may look like this:

Sex Grade 7 Grade 8 Grade 9 Grade 10 Total
Male 8 6 7 7 28
Female 7 6 4 5 22
Total 15 12 11 12 50

How to Interpret the Results

Read the table one cell at a time. Each cell tells you the number of students belonging to a particular combination of Sex and Grade Level.

Example:

Male Grade 7 Frequency = 8

Interpretation:

There are 8 male students in Grade 7.


Female Grade 10 Frequency = 5

Interpretation:

There are 5 female students in Grade 10.

Reading the Totals

The totals provide additional information.

  • The last column shows the total number of Male and Female students.
  • The last row shows the total number of students in each Grade Level.
  • The bottom-right corner shows the total number of respondents in the study.

How to Describe the Results in Words

The Crosstab shows the distribution of students according to Sex and Grade Level. There are 8 male students and 7 female students in Grade 7. Grade 8 consists of 6 male students and 6 female students. Overall, the study includes 28 male students and 22 female students.
Professor's Notes
  • The professor used the Crosstabs procedure to answer this question.
  • Place Sex in the Rows box and Grade Level in the Columns box.
  • The Crosstab summarizes two categorical variables in a single table, making comparison easier.
Common Mistakes
  • Using Frequencies instead of Crosstabs.
  • Placing Mathematical Ability or English Proficiency in the Rows or Columns boxes.
  • Confusing the row totals with the column totals.
  • Interpreting the totals incorrectly instead of reading the individual cells first.

Question 7

What is the Mean and Standard Deviation of Mathematical Ability According to Sex?

What is this question asking?

This question asks you to compare the average Mathematical Ability scores of male and female students. Instead of looking at all students as one group, the scores are separated according to Sex.

SPSS will also compute the Standard Deviation for each group to determine how consistent the students' scores are.

Why is this important?

This analysis allows you to compare the performance of two groups. It answers questions such as:

  • Which group has the higher average Mathematical Ability score?
  • Which group has more consistent scores?
  • Are the scores of one group more spread out than the other?

Take note that this question only asks for the Mean and Standard Deviation. It does not ask whether the difference is statistically significant. That will be answered later using the Independent Samples t-test.

SPSS Procedure

Step 1

Click
Analyze -> Compare Means -> Means
Step 2

Select
Mathematical Ability
Move it to the Dependent List box.
Step 3

Select
Sex
Move it to the Independent List box.
Step 4

Click
Options
Make sure the following are selected:
  • Mean
  • Standard Deviation
  • Number of Cases (N)
Click
Continue
Step 5

Click
OK

What Will SPSS Display?

SPSS will generate a table similar to the one below.

Sex N Mean Standard Deviation
Male 28 84.25 4.15
Female 22 82.40 5.31
Total 50 83.44 4.73

Understanding Each Column

Column Meaning
N The number of students in each group.
Mean The average Mathematical Ability score.
Standard Deviation Shows how spread out the scores are within each group.

How to Interpret the Mean

Compare the Mean of the two groups.

The group with the higher Mean has the higher average Mathematical Ability score.

Example:

Male Mean = 84.25 Female Mean = 82.40

Interpretation:

Male students obtained a higher average Mathematical Ability score than female students.

How to Interpret the Standard Deviation

The Standard Deviation tells you how close the students' scores are to their group average.

  • A lower Standard Deviation means the scores are more consistent.
  • A higher Standard Deviation means the scores are more spread out.

Example:

Male SD = 4.15 Female SD = 5.31

Interpretation:

The male students have more consistent Mathematical Ability scores because their Standard Deviation is lower. The female students have more variation in their scores because their Standard Deviation is higher.

How to Describe the Results in Words

Male students obtained a mean Mathematical Ability score of 84.25 (SD = 4.15), while female students obtained a mean score of 82.40 (SD = 5.31). Based on the descriptive statistics, male students have a slightly higher average score, while their scores are also more consistent.
Professor's Notes
  • The professor emphasized comparing the Mean first.
  • The group with the higher Mean has the better average performance.
  • The group with the lower Standard Deviation has more consistent scores.
  • This analysis only describes the groups. It does not determine whether the difference is statistically significant.
Common Mistakes
  • Thinking that a higher Standard Deviation means better performance.
  • Confusing Mean with Standard Deviation.
  • Concluding that one group is significantly better based only on the Mean. Statistical significance is tested later using the Independent Samples t-test.
  • Ignoring the Standard Deviation when comparing the groups.

Parametric Analysis - Question 2

Is there a Significant Difference Between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency?

Statistical Test: Paired Samples t-test (Dependent Samples t-test)

What is this question asking?

This question asks whether there is a significant difference between the students' Mathematical Ability scores and their English Proficiency scores. Since both scores belong to the same students, the two sets of scores are paired or related.

The Paired Samples t-test compares the average of the two related variables to determine whether they differ significantly.

When is the Paired Samples t-test Used?

Use the Paired Samples t-test when:

  • The same respondents are measured twice.
  • Each person has two related scores.
  • The variables are measured using a numerical (Scale) level.

For this problem:

Variable 1 Variable 2
Mathematical Ability English Proficiency

Hypotheses

Null Hypothesis (H0)

There is no significant difference between the Mathematical Ability scores and English Proficiency scores.

Alternative Hypothesis (H1)

There is a significant difference between the Mathematical Ability scores and English Proficiency scores.

Level of Significance

alpha = 0.05

Decision Rule

  • If Sig. (2-tailed) <= 0.05, reject the Null Hypothesis.
  • If Sig. (2-tailed) > 0.05, fail to reject the Null Hypothesis.

SPSS Procedure

Step 1

Click
Analyze -> Compare Means -> Paired-Samples T Test
Step 2

Select

Move them together into the Paired Variables box.

How to Interpret the Results

Look only at the value of Sig. (2-tailed).

Sig. (2-tailed) Decision
<= 0.05 Reject the Null Hypothesis.
> 0.05 Fail to reject the Null Hypothesis.

Example Interpretation

Sig. (2-tailed) = 0.034 Since 0.034 is less than 0.05, reject the Null Hypothesis. There is a significant difference between the Mathematical Ability scores and English Proficiency scores.

How to Write the Conclusion

A Paired Samples t-test was conducted to determine whether there is a significant difference between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency. The analysis produced a Sig. (2-tailed) value of 0.034, which is less than the 0.05 level of significance. Therefore, the Null Hypothesis is rejected. This indicates that there is a statistically significant difference between the Mathematical Ability scores and English Proficiency scores of the students.
Professor's Notes
  • Use the Paired Samples t-test only when both scores belong to the same respondents.
  • The final decision is based on the Sig. (2-tailed) value in the Paired Samples Test table.
  • The Paired Samples Statistics table is descriptive only and should not be used to determine statistical significance.
  • If the Sig. (2-tailed) value is less than or equal to 0.05, conclude that there is a significant difference between the two variables.

Parametric Analysis - Question 3

Is there a Significant Difference in Mathematical Ability According to Grade Level?

Statistical Test: One-Way ANOVA

Purpose

Determines whether the average Mathematical Ability scores differ among the different grade levels.

Hypotheses

H0: There is no significant difference in Mathematical Ability according to Grade Level. H1: There is a significant difference in Mathematical Ability according to Grade Level.

SPSS Procedure

Analyze -> Compare Means -> One-Way ANOVA
  • Dependent List: Mathematical Ability
  • Factor: Grade Level
  • Click OK

Interpretation

Sig. Value Decision
<= 0.05 Reject H0. There is a significant difference.
> 0.05 Fail to reject H0. There is no significant difference.

Example

Sig. = 0.018 Since 0.018 is less than 0.05, reject the Null Hypothesis. There is a significant difference in Mathematical Ability according to Grade Level.

ANOVA only tells whether a difference exists. It does not identify which grade levels differ. A Post Hoc test is needed for that.

Parametric Analysis - Question 4

Is there a Significant Relationship Between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency?

Statistical Test: Pearson Product-Moment Correlation (Pearson r)

Purpose

Determines whether Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency are related.

Hypotheses

H0: There is no significant relationship between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency. H1: There is a significant relationship between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency.

SPSS Procedure

Analyze -> Correlate -> Bivariate
  • Select Mathematical Ability
  • Select English Proficiency
  • Check Pearson
  • Check Two-tailed
  • Click OK

Interpretation

Look at two values:

  • Pearson Correlation (r) - indicates the strength and direction of the relationship.
  • Sig. (2-tailed) - determines whether the relationship is statistically significant.
Sig. Value Decision
<= 0.05 Reject H0. There is a significant relationship.
> 0.05 Fail to reject H0. There is no significant relationship.

Example

Pearson r = 0.72 Sig. = 0.001 Since 0.001 is less than 0.05, reject the Null Hypothesis. There is a significant positive relationship between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency.
Professor's Note

The sign of Pearson r (+ or -) shows the direction of the relationship, while the Sig. value determines whether the relationship is statistically significant.
