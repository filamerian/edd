T-Test for Dependent Samples

Comparing Two Related Groups or Repeated Measures

Introduction

In research, there are situations where the same group of participants is measured twice, or two related conditions are compared. For example, students may take a pre-test and post-test after an intervention.

The t-test for dependent samples is used when the two sets of data are related or paired in some way.


Learning Objectives

  • Define the dependent samples t-test.
  • Identify when to use paired sample analysis.
  • Explain the assumptions of the test.
  • Compute and interpret results of paired comparisons.

Historical Background

The dependent samples t-test evolved from early work on paired observations in agricultural and medical research, where the same subjects were measured before and after treatment.

William Sealy Gosset ("Student")'s work on the t-distribution also laid the foundation for paired comparisons, especially when sample sizes are small.


Definition

The dependent samples t-test is used to determine whether there is a significant difference between two related means, such as pre-test and post-test scores.


When to Use the Dependent Samples T-Test

  • Same group measured twice (pre-test/post-test).
  • Matched or paired participants.
  • Continuous data (interval or ratio scale).
  • Differences between pairs are approximately normally distributed.

T-Test Formula

t = d̄ / (sd / sqrt(n))

Where:

  • d̄ = mean of the differences
  • sd = standard deviation of the differences
  • n = number of paired observations

Assumptions

  • The pairs are dependent (related observations).
  • The differences are normally distributed.
  • Data is continuous.
  • Observations are randomly selected.

Worked Example

Research Scenario

A teacher wants to determine whether a new teaching strategy improves student performance. The same group of students takes a pre-test and a post-test.

Given Information
  • Mean Difference (d̄) = 6
  • Standard Deviation of Differences (sd) = 5
  • Number of Students (n) = 25

Solution

Step 1 - State the Hypotheses

We test whether there is a significant difference between the pre-test and post-test scores.

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no significant difference between the paired means.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a significant difference between the paired means.
H0: d̄ = 0
H1: d̄ != 0
Step 2 - Identify the Formula
t = d̄ / (sd / sqrt(n))
Step 3 - Substitute the Given Values
  • d̄ = 6
  • sd = 5
  • n = 25

Substituting:

t = 6 / (5 / sqrt(25))

Step 4 - Compute the Values

sqrt(25) = 5

5 / 5 = 1

t = 6 / 1

t = 6.00

Step 5 - Decision Rule

At a = 0.05 (two-tailed test), degrees of freedom:

df = n - 1 = 25 - 1 = 24

Critical value:

t critical = ±2.064 (approx)

Decision rule:

  • If |t computed| > t critical, reject H0
  • If |t computed| = t critical, fail to reject H0
Step 6 - Decision
t = 6.00

Since 6.00 is greater than 2.064, we reject the null hypothesis.

Step 7 - Interpretation

There is a significant difference between pre-test and post-test scores. This suggests that the teaching strategy had a positive effect on student performance.


Applications

  • Pre-test and post-test educational studies
  • Medical treatment effectiveness
  • Behavioral intervention studies
  • Training program evaluation

Summary

The dependent samples t-test is used for comparing two related measurements. In this example, the computed t-value (6.00) exceeded the critical value (±2.064), leading to rejection of the null hypothesis and indicating a significant difference between paired observations.
