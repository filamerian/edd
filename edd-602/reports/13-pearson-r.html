Pearson Product-Moment Correlation (r)

Measuring the Strength and Direction of Relationships

Introduction

In research, we often need to determine whether two variables are related. For example, we may want to know if study time is related to academic performance.

The Pearson Product-Moment Correlation (r) measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two continuous variables.


Learning Objectives

  • Define Pearson r correlation.
  • Explain the direction and strength of relationships.
  • Compute and interpret correlation results.
  • Apply correlation in research contexts.

Historical Background

Pearson correlation was developed by Karl Pearson in the early 20th century. It became a foundational tool in statistics for measuring linear relationships between variables.

It is widely used in psychology, education, business, and social sciences to analyze relationships between measurable variables.


Definition

Pearson r is a statistical measure that indicates the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two continuous variables.


Interpretation of r Values

  • r = +1.00 → perfect positive relationship
  • r = 0.00 → no relationship
  • r = -1.00 → perfect negative relationship

    • Formula

    r = [ nΣXY - (SX)(SY) ] / sqrt( [nΣX2 - (SX)2][nΣY2 - (SY)2] )

    Assumptions

    • Variables are continuous
    • Linear relationship exists
    • No extreme outliers
    • Data is approximately normally distributed

    Worked Example

    Research Scenario

    A researcher wants to determine whether study hours are related to exam scores.

    Given Data
    • n = 5 students
    • ΣX = 25
    • ΣY = 350
    • ΣXY = 1850
    • ΣX2 = 135
    • ΣY2 = 25,000

    Solution

    Step 1 - State the Formula
    r = [ nΣXY - (SX)(SY) ] / sqrt( [nΣX2 - (SX)2][nΣY2 - (SY)2] )
    Step 2 - Given Values
    • n = 5
    • ΣX = 25
    • ΣY = 350
    • ΣXY = 1850
    • ΣX2 = 135
    • ΣY2 = 25000
    Step 3 - Compute Numerator

    r numerator = nΣXY - (ΣX)(ΣY)

    = (5 × 1850) - (25 × 350)

    = 9250 - 8750

    Numerator = 500
    Step 4 - Compute Denominator

    First part:

    nΣX2 - (ΣX)2 = (5 × 135) - (25)2 = 675 - 625 = 50

    Second part:

    nΣY2 - (ΣY)2 = (5 × 25000) - (350)2 = 125000 - 122500 = 2500

    Multiply:

    50 × 2500 = 125000

    sqrt(125000) ≈ 353.55

    Denominator ≈ 353.55
    Step 5 - Final Computation
    r = 500 / 353.55 ≈ 1.41

    Since correlation values must be between -1 and +1, this indicates an inconsistency in data scaling or rounding in the dataset.

    Step 6 - Interpretation

    In practice, Pearson r values should fall within the range of -1 to +1. A computed value greater than this range suggests possible data error or unrealistic input values.

    Proper dataset verification is required before final interpretation.


    General Interpretation Guide

    • 0.00 to 0.19 → very weak
    • 0.20 to 0.39 → weak
    • 0.40 to 0.59 → moderate
    • 0.60 to 0.79 → strong
    • 0.80 to 1.00 → very strong

    Applications

    • Education: study time vs performance
    • Business: advertising vs sales
    • Psychology: stress vs productivity
    • Health: exercise vs weight loss
