Z-Test for Sample and Population Means

Comparing a Sample Mean with a Known Population Mean

Introduction

In research, we often need to determine whether a sample mean is significantly different from a known population mean. This situation commonly occurs when researchers want to evaluate performance, outcomes, or measurements against an established standard.

The Z-test for sample and population means is a statistical method used when the population standard deviation is known and the sample size is large (typically n = 30).


Learning Objectives

  • Define the Z-test for a single sample mean.
  • Identify when to use the Z-test.
  • Explain the assumptions of the Z-test.
  • Compute and interpret Z-test results.

Historical Background

The Z-test is rooted in the development of the normal distribution by Carl Friedrich Gauss in the 18th and 19th centuries. The standard normal distribution, often called the Z-distribution, became a foundation for modern inferential statistics.

Later, statisticians formalized the use of Z-scores to compare individual or sample values against a population distribution. This method became widely used in education, psychology, and quality control research to evaluate whether observed differences are statistically significant.


Definition

The Z-test for a single sample mean is a statistical test used to determine whether the mean of a sample differs significantly from a known population mean when the population standard deviation is known.


When to Use the Z-Test

  • The population standard deviation (s) is known.
  • The sample size is large (n = 30).
  • The data is approximately normally distributed or the sample is large enough.
  • You are comparing a sample mean to a known population mean.

Z-Test Formula

Z = (X̄ - μ) / (σ / √n)

Where:

  • X̄ = sample mean
  • μ = population mean
  • σ = population standard deviation
  • n = sample size

Assumptions

  • The sample is randomly selected.
  • The population standard deviation is known.
  • The sample size is sufficiently large (n = 30).
  • The observations are independent.

Worked Example

Research Scenario

A researcher wants to determine whether the average score of students in a statistics class is different from the university's known average performance score.

The university has a known population mean score, and the researcher collects a sample of students to compare their performance against this benchmark.

Given Information
  • Population Mean (μ) = 75
  • Population Standard Deviation (σ) = 10
  • Sample Mean (X̄) = 78
  • Sample Size (n) = 40

Solution

Step 1 - State the Hypotheses

Before computing the Z-test, we establish the hypotheses.

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): There is no significant difference between the sample mean and the population mean.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): There is a significant difference between the sample mean and the population mean.
H0: X̄ = μ
H1: X̄ != μ
Step 2 - Identify the Formula

We use the Z-test formula for a single sample mean:

Z = (X̄ - μ) / (σ / √n)
Step 3 - Substitute the Given Values

Given:

  • X̄ = 78
  • μ = 75
  • σ = 10
  • n = 40

Substitute into the formula:

Z = (78 - 75) / (10 / v40)

Step 4 - Compute the Values

First compute the numerator:

78 - 75 = 3

Next compute the denominator:

v40 = 6.32 (approx)
10 / 6.32 = 1.58 (approx)

Now compute Z:

Z = 3 / 1.58

Z = 1.90 (approx)

Step 5 - Decision Rule

At a = 0.05 (two-tailed test), the critical values are:

Z critical = ±1.96

Decision rule:

  • If |Z computed| > 1.96, reject H0
  • If |Z computed| = 1.96, fail to reject H0
Step 6 - Decision

The computed Z value is:

Z = 1.90

Since 1.90 is less than 1.96, we fail to reject the null hypothesis.

Step 7 - Interpretation

There is no sufficient evidence to conclude that the mean score of the sample is significantly different from the population mean of 75.

This suggests that the students' performance is statistically similar to the university's expected average.


Applications

  • Comparing student performance to institutional standards
  • Quality control in manufacturing processes
  • Educational assessment evaluation
  • Benchmark testing in organizations

Summary

The Z-test for a single sample mean is used to compare a sample mean against a known population mean when the population standard deviation is known. In this example, the computed value of Z = 1.90 was less than the critical value of ±1.96, leading to the conclusion that there is no significant difference between the sample and population means.
