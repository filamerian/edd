Taro Yamane's Formula

Sample Size Determination for Finite Populations

Introduction

Determining the correct sample size is one of the most important steps in quantitative research. A sample that is too small may not represent the population, while a sample that is too large may require unnecessary time and resources.

Taro Yamane's Formula provides a simple and practical method for determining the required sample size when the total population is known.


Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of Taro Yamane's Formula.
  • Identify the variables used in the formula.
  • Compute the required sample size.
  • Interpret the computed result.

Historical Background

Taro Yamane introduced this formula in his book Statistics: An Introductory Analysis published in 1967.

Because of its simplicity, the formula has become one of the most commonly used methods for determining sample size in educational, business, and social science research.


Definition

Taro Yamane's Formula is a statistical formula used to estimate the minimum sample size needed from a known population while maintaining a desired margin of error.


Formula

n = N / (1 + N(e2))


Variables

Symbol Description
n Required sample size
N Total population size
e Margin of error expressed as a decimal

Worked Example

Given:
  • Population (N) = 2500
  • Margin of Error (e) = 0.05

Step 1

n = 2500 / (1 + 2500(0.052))

Step 2

0.052 = 0.0025

Step 3

2500 x 0.0025 = 6.25

Step 4

1 + 6.25 = 7.25

Step 5

2500 / 7.25 = 344.83

Required Sample Size = 345 Respondents

Applications

  • Educational Research
  • Business Research
  • Market Research
  • Community Surveys
  • Government Research

Advantages

  • Simple to understand.
  • Easy to compute manually.
  • Suitable for finite populations.
  • Widely accepted by researchers.

Limitations

  • Population size must be known.
  • Assumes random sampling.
  • Does not account for population variability.

Summary

Taro Yamane's Formula provides researchers with a quick and practical way to determine sample size for finite populations. It is commonly used in educational research because of its simplicity and ease of application.
