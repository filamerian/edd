Jacob Cohen's Formula

Sample Size Determination Using Effect Size and Statistical Power

Introduction

Jacob Cohen's approach to sample size determination is based on the concept of statistical power. Rather than relying only on the size of the population, Cohen proposed that researchers should also consider the expected effect size, significance level, and desired statistical power when deciding how many participants are needed.

Today, Cohen's method is widely used in educational, psychological, and social science research. Modern statistical software such as G*Power is built upon the principles introduced by Cohen.


Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of Cohen's sample size approach.
  • Define effect size, significance level, and statistical power.
  • Interpret Cohen's guidelines for effect size.
  • Estimate an appropriate sample size for a research study.

Historical Background

Jacob Cohen (1923-1998) was an American statistician and psychologist who made significant contributions to statistical analysis in behavioral sciences. His book, Statistical Power Analysis for the Behavioral Sciences, introduced practical methods for determining sample size based on statistical power and effect size.

Cohen emphasized that researchers should not only determine whether a result is statistically significant but should also ensure that their study includes enough participants to detect meaningful differences or relationships.


Definition

Cohen's approach determines the required sample size by considering four important factors:

  • Effect Size
  • Significance Level (Alpha)
  • Statistical Power
  • Type of Statistical Test

Unlike Taro Yamane or Cochran, Cohen's method does not rely on a single equation. Instead, the required sample size depends on the statistical procedure that will be used in the study.


Key Concepts

Concept Description
Effect Size The magnitude of the difference or relationship expected in the study.
Alpha (a) The probability of making a Type I Error. A value of 0.05 is commonly used.
Statistical Power The probability of detecting a true effect. A value of 0.80 is generally recommended.
Sample Size The number of participants required to achieve the desired statistical power.

Cohen's Effect Size Guidelines

Effect Size Cohen's d Interpretation
Small 0.20 A small but noticeable difference.
Medium 0.50 A moderate difference that is commonly expected.
Large 0.80 A substantial difference between groups.

Worked Example

Research Scenario

A doctoral student at Filamer Christian University, Inc. wants to determine whether an interactive teaching strategy improves the statistics performance of graduate students compared with the traditional lecture method.

The researcher plans to compare the scores of two independent groups using an Independent Samples t-Test.

Research Decisions
Parameter Selected Value
Significance Level (Alpha) 0.05
Statistical Power 0.80
Expected Effect Size 0.50 (Medium)
Step 1 - Identify the Statistical Test

The researcher wants to compare the mean scores of two independent groups: one group taught using the traditional lecture method and another group taught using an interactive teaching strategy.

Statistical Test: Independent Samples t-Test
Step 2 - Select the Significance Level

The researcher selects an alpha level of 0.05. This means there is a 5 percent chance of concluding that the new teaching strategy is effective when it is actually not.

Alpha = 0.05
Step 3 - Choose the Statistical Power

The researcher wants to have an 80 percent chance of detecting a real difference if the interactive teaching strategy truly improves student performance.

Statistical Power = 0.80
Step 4 - Estimate the Effect Size

Since no previous study is available, the researcher follows Cohen's recommendation and assumes a medium effect size (0.50). This represents a moderate difference between the two teaching methods.

Effect Size (Cohen's d) = 0.50
Step 5 - Determine the Sample Size

Using Cohen's sample size tables or software such as G*Power, the recommended sample size for an Independent Samples t-Test with these parameters is approximately:

64 Participants per Group

Since there are two groups, the total required sample size is:

Total Sample Size = 128 Participants


Interpretation

The researcher should recruit approximately 128 graduate students, with 64 students assigned to each teaching method.

This sample size provides sufficient statistical power to detect a medium-sized difference between the two teaching strategies at the 0.05 significance level.

If fewer participants are included, the study may fail to detect an actual difference even when one truly exists.


Applications

  • Educational research
  • Experimental studies
  • Psychological research
  • Behavioral science
  • Medical and health research
  • Graduate thesis and dissertation studies

Advantages

  • Produces scientifically justified sample sizes.
  • Considers the expected magnitude of the effect.
  • Reduces the likelihood of conducting underpowered studies.
  • Applicable to many statistical procedures.
  • Forms the basis of modern power analysis software.

Limitations

  • Requires researchers to estimate the expected effect size.
  • Different statistical tests require different calculations.
  • Usually performed using statistical tables or specialized software.
  • May be difficult for beginning researchers without prior statistical knowledge.

Summary

Jacob Cohen introduced an approach to determining sample size based on statistical power rather than population size alone. By considering the significance level, effect size, statistical power, and research design, researchers can collect enough data to detect meaningful differences or relationships. Cohen's concepts remain the foundation of modern sample size analysis and are widely implemented in software such as G*Power.
