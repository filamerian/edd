EdD 602 - Advanced Educational Statistics

Levels of Measurement

Learning Objectives

At the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Define the four levels of measurement.
  • Differentiate nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio scales.
  • Identify the level of measurement of research variables.
  • Select appropriate statistical procedures based on the level of measurement.
  • Avoid common mistakes in classifying research data.

What are Levels of Measurement?

Levels of measurement refer to the way data are classified and measured. They determine the amount of information that a variable contains and influence the choice of statistical procedures that may be applied.

Before performing any statistical analysis, a researcher must first determine the level of measurement of every variable in the study. Using an inappropriate statistical test often results from incorrectly identifying the measurement scale.

Remember: The level of measurement determines which statistical techniques are appropriate for analyzing the data.

The Four Levels of Measurement

In quantitative research, variables are commonly classified into four levels of measurement introduced by psychologist Stanley Stevens.

Level Main Characteristic Examples
Nominal Classification into categories only. Gender, Religion, Program, Civil Status
Ordinal Classification with ranking or order. Class Rank, Satisfaction Rating, Socioeconomic Status
Interval Equal intervals but no true zero. Temperature (Celsius), IQ Score
Ratio Equal intervals with an absolute zero. Age, Height, Weight, Income, Test Score

Why are Levels of Measurement Important?

The level of measurement determines how data may be summarized, analyzed, and interpreted. Some statistical procedures require interval or ratio data, while others are specifically designed for nominal or ordinal variables.

For example, Pearson correlation requires interval or ratio data, whereas Chi-Square analysis is commonly used for nominal variables.

Measurement Level Common Statistical Procedures
Nominal Frequency, Percentage, Chi-Square
Ordinal Median, Spearman Correlation, Mann-Whitney U Test
Interval Mean, Standard Deviation, t-Test, ANOVA, Pearson Correlation
Ratio All parametric statistical procedures

1. Nominal Scale

The nominal scale is the simplest level of measurement. It classifies observations into categories without implying any order or ranking.

Numbers assigned to nominal data are merely labels. They do not indicate quantity or magnitude.

Examples
  • Gender (Male, Female)
  • Civil Status
  • Religion
  • Academic Program
  • Blood Type
Important: If "Male" is coded as 1 and "Female" as 2, this does not mean that 2 is greater than 1. The numbers serve only as category labels.

2. Ordinal Scale

The ordinal scale classifies observations into categories that have a meaningful order or ranking. However, the intervals between categories are not necessarily equal.

Ordinal data indicate relative position but do not measure the exact difference between ranks.

Examples
  • Class Rank
  • Likert Scale Responses
  • Employee Performance Ratings
  • Socioeconomic Status

Consider the following satisfaction ratings:

Rating Meaning
5 Strongly Agree
4 Agree
3 Neutral
2 Disagree
1 Strongly Disagree

Although 5 represents a higher level of agreement than 4, the difference between consecutive ratings cannot be assumed to be exactly equal.


3. Interval Scale

The interval scale possesses all the characteristics of the ordinal scale, but the intervals between values are equal and meaningful. However, an interval scale does not have a true or absolute zero.

Because there is no true zero, statements involving multiplication or division are not meaningful. For example, a temperature of 40°C is not "twice as hot" as 20°C.

Examples
  • Temperature measured in Celsius
  • Temperature measured in Fahrenheit
  • Intelligence Quotient (IQ)
  • Calendar Years

Interval data allow researchers to compute the mean, standard deviation, correlation, and many other parametric statistical procedures.


4. Ratio Scale

The ratio scale is the highest level of measurement. It possesses all the characteristics of the interval scale but also includes a true zero, indicating the complete absence of the measured quantity.

Because ratio data have an absolute zero, comparisons involving multiplication and division are meaningful.

Examples
  • Age
  • Height
  • Weight
  • Monthly Income
  • Years of Teaching Experience
  • Number of Books in a Library
  • Examination Scores
Example: A person who is 40 years old is twice as old as someone who is 20 years old. This comparison is valid because age has a true zero.

Comparison of the Four Levels of Measurement

Characteristic Nominal Ordinal Interval Ratio
Categories Yes Yes Yes Yes
Ranking No Yes Yes Yes
Equal Intervals No No Yes Yes
True Zero No No No Yes
Parametric Statistics No Limited Yes Yes

How to Identify the Level of Measurement

  1. Ask whether the data simply classify observations into groups.
    If yes, the level is Nominal.

  2. If the data can be ranked, determine whether the differences between ranks are equal.
    If not, the level is Ordinal.

  3. If equal intervals exist, determine whether the variable has a true zero.
    If there is no true zero, the level is Interval.

  4. If equal intervals exist and the variable has a true zero, the level is Ratio.

Common Mistakes

  • Treating nominal data as numerical values.
  • Assuming every Likert scale is interval data.
  • Confusing interval data with ratio data.
  • Selecting statistical tests without considering the measurement level.
  • Believing that every variable measured with numbers is ratio data.

Lesson Summary

  • Levels of measurement determine how data are classified and analyzed.
  • Nominal data classify observations into categories.
  • Ordinal data classify observations and establish rankings.
  • Interval data have equal intervals but no true zero.
  • Ratio data have equal intervals and an absolute zero.
  • Correctly identifying the measurement level helps researchers choose appropriate statistical procedures.

Self-Check Questions

  1. Differentiate the four levels of measurement.
  2. Why is identifying the measurement level important before statistical analysis?
  3. Classify the following variables: gender, age, class rank, monthly income, and temperature in Celsius.
  4. Why is IQ considered interval data instead of ratio data?
  5. Which statistical procedures are commonly used with nominal variables?
