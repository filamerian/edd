Population, Sample, and Sampling Techniques

Learning Objectives

At the end of this lesson, you should be able to:

  • Define population and sample.
  • Differentiate population from sample.
  • Explain why sampling is important in research.
  • Differentiate probability and non-probability sampling techniques.
  • Select the most appropriate sampling technique for a research study.

Introduction

One of the first decisions a researcher makes is determining who will participate in the study. Although it is ideal to collect data from every member of the population, doing so is often impractical because of limited time, budget, and available resources.

For this reason, researchers usually collect data from a smaller group called a sample. If the sample is properly selected, it can accurately represent the characteristics of the entire population.

Remember: The quality of research findings depends greatly on how the sample is selected.

What is a Population?

A population is the complete group of individuals, objects, or events that possess the characteristics being studied. It is the entire group about which the researcher wants to draw conclusions.

Populations may be very small, such as all teachers in one school, or extremely large, such as all public school teachers in the Philippines.

Examples of Populations
  • All Grade 12 students of a university.
  • All public elementary school teachers in Capiz.
  • All MBA students enrolled during Academic Year 2026-2027.
  • All registered nurses working in a provincial hospital.

What is a Sample?

A sample is a subset of the population selected to participate in the study. The purpose of sampling is to obtain information that represents the entire population.

If the sample accurately reflects the characteristics of the population, the findings may be generalized to the entire group.

Example

Population: All 2,500 undergraduate students of a university.

Sample: 333 students selected using stratified random sampling.

Population versus Sample

Population Sample
Entire group being studied. Part of the population.
Usually very large. Smaller and easier to study.
Produces parameters. Produces statistics.
Often expensive and time-consuming to study. Less expensive and more practical.
Represents everyone of interest. Represents the population.

Why Do Researchers Use Sampling?

Researchers use sampling because studying every member of a population is often impossible. Sampling allows researchers to obtain reliable information while reducing costs, time, and effort.

Reason Explanation
Saves Time Collecting data from a sample is much faster than surveying an entire population.
Reduces Cost Fewer respondents require fewer materials, personnel, and resources.
More Practical Large populations are often impossible to study completely.
Produces Reliable Results Properly selected samples can accurately represent the population.

Two Major Categories of Sampling Techniques

Sampling techniques are generally classified into two major categories depending on how participants are selected.

Category Description
Probability Sampling Every member of the population has a known chance of being selected.
Non-Probability Sampling Participants are selected without randomization.

Probability sampling generally produces findings that can be generalized to the population, whereas non-probability sampling is commonly used in exploratory, qualitative, or difficult-to-reach populations.


Probability Sampling Techniques

Probability sampling uses random selection so that every member of the population has a known probability of being included in the study.

This approach minimizes selection bias and is the preferred method for most quantitative research studies.

Technique Description
Simple Random Sampling Every member has an equal chance of being selected.
Systematic Sampling Every kth member is selected after a random starting point.
Stratified Sampling The population is divided into strata, then random samples are drawn from each group.
Cluster Sampling Entire groups or clusters are randomly selected instead of individual participants.

Non-Probability Sampling Techniques

Non-probability sampling does not use random selection. Instead, participants are chosen based on availability, judgment, specific characteristics, or willingness to participate.

Although these techniques are easier and less expensive to use, they generally have limited ability to represent the entire population.

Technique Description
Convenience Sampling Participants are selected because they are easy to reach.
Purposive Sampling Participants are selected because they possess specific characteristics needed for the study.
Quota Sampling Participants are selected until a required number from each category has been reached.
Snowball Sampling Existing participants recommend additional participants who meet the study requirements.

Probability versus Non-Probability Sampling

Feature Probability Sampling Non-Probability Sampling
Selection Method Random selection. Non-random selection.
Chance of Selection Known. Unknown.
Selection Bias Lower. Higher.
Generalization Usually possible. Usually limited.
Common Use Quantitative Research. Qualitative and Exploratory Research.

How to Choose an Appropriate Sampling Technique

Selecting the proper sampling technique depends on the research objectives, available resources, accessibility of participants, and desired level of generalization.

If Your Goal Is... Recommended Technique
Give every participant an equal chance of selection. Simple Random Sampling
Represent several subgroups fairly. Stratified Sampling
Select schools, classrooms, or communities. Cluster Sampling
Reach participants who are readily available. Convenience Sampling
Select participants with specialized expertise. Purposive Sampling
Reach difficult-to-locate participants. Snowball Sampling

Examples in Educational Research

Research Topic Possible Population Possible Sample Recommended Sampling Technique
Students' Readiness for Online Learning All undergraduate students 350 students Stratified Random Sampling
Leadership Practices of School Principals All public school principals 120 principals Simple Random Sampling
Experiences of Outstanding Teachers Outstanding teachers 20 award-winning teachers Purposive Sampling
Graduate Students' Satisfaction All graduate students 250 graduate students Systematic Sampling

Common Mistakes in Sampling

  • Selecting participants based only on convenience when random sampling is feasible.
  • Using a sample that is too small to represent the population.
  • Ignoring important subgroups within the population.
  • Confusing the population with the sample.
  • Using non-probability sampling while claiming the results represent the entire population.

Lesson Summary

  • A population is the complete group that the researcher wants to study.
  • A sample is a subset selected from the population.
  • Sampling saves time, cost, and effort while providing reliable information.
  • Probability sampling uses random selection and supports statistical generalization.
  • Non-probability sampling does not use random selection and is commonly used in exploratory studies.
  • The choice of sampling technique depends on the research objectives and the characteristics of the population.

Self-Check Questions

  3. Differentiate probability sampling from non-probability sampling.
