Course Description

EdD 602 - Advanced Educational Statistics provides doctoral students with advanced knowledge and practical skills in selecting, computing, interpreting, and reporting statistical procedures commonly used in educational research.

This website serves as a learning resource containing lesson notes, worked examples, and references for each statistical technique discussed in the course.


SPSS Tutorial: Descriptive, Parametric, and Non-Parametric Analysis

General Questions

  1. Create a frequency distribution for Mathematical Ability.
  2. Compute the Mean, Median, and Mode of English Proficiency.
  3. Compute the Standard Deviation (SD), Variance, and Range of Mathematical Ability.
  4. Compute the Kurtosis and Skewness of English Proficiency.
  5. Create the frequency distribution of Sex and Grade Level.
  6. Create the frequency distribution of Sex according to Grade Level.
  7. Compute the Mean and Standard Deviation (SD) of Mathematical Ability according to Sex.
  8. Compute the Mean and Standard Deviation (SD) of Mathematical Ability according to Grade Level.
  9. Compute the Mean and Standard Deviation (SD) of Mathematical Ability according to Sex and Grade Level.

Parametric Analysis

Assumption: The distribution of the data is normal.

  1. Is there a significant difference in Mathematical Ability according to Sex? (Independent Samples t-test)
  2. Is there a significant difference between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency? (Paired Samples t-test)
  3. Is there a significant difference in Mathematical Ability according to Grade Levels? (One-Way ANOVA)
  4. Is there a significant relationship between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency? (Pearson Product-Moment Correlation / Pearson r)

Non-Parametric Analysis

Assumption: The distribution of the data is not normal.

  1. Is there a significant difference in Mathematical Ability according to Sex? (Mann-Whitney U Test)
  2. Is there a significant difference between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency? (Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test)
  3. Is there a significant difference in Mathematical Ability according to Grade Levels? (Kruskal-Wallis H Test)
  4. Is there a significant relationship between Mathematical Ability and English Proficiency? (Spearman's Rank Correlation / Spearman's rho)

Module 4 - Nonparametric Tests

Nonparametric tests are used when data do not follow a normal distribution or when the assumptions required for parametric tests are not met. These tests are useful for ranked data, ordinal data, or small sample sizes.


Overview of Reports (16 to 20)

Report Description Formula & Interpretation Application
16 Chi-Square Test of Independence χ2 = Σ (O - E)2 / E

Interpretation: A significant result means there is a relationship between two variables. 		Used to check if two categorical variables are related.

Example: Checking if gender is related to preferred learning style in students.
17 Chi-Square Goodness of Fit χ2 = Σ (O - E)2 / E

Interpretation: A significant result means the observed data distribution is different from the expected distribution. 		Used to compare observed frequencies with expected patterns.

Example: Checking if student grades follow an expected distribution pattern.
18 Mann-Whitney U Test U = n1n2 + (n1(n1+1)/2) - R1

Interpretation: A significant result means there is a difference between two independent groups. 		Used to compare two independent groups.

Example: Comparing exam scores of students taught using two different teaching methods.
19 Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test T = Σ ranks of differences

Interpretation: A significant result means there is a difference between paired measurements. 		Used to compare two related samples.

Example: Comparing students' pre-test and post-test scores after an intervention.
20 Kruskal-Wallis H Test H = (12 / n(n+1)) Σ(Ri2 / ni) - 3(n+1)

Interpretation: A significant result means at least one group is different from the others. 		Used to compare three or more independent groups.

Example: Comparing student performance across three different schools.

Module 5 - Advanced Analysis of Variance

Advanced Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) is used to test differences among group means when there are three or more groups. It helps determine whether at least one group is significantly different from the others.

This module includes advanced variations of ANOVA such as post hoc tests and multiple comparison procedures.


Overview of Reports (21 to 24 and 33 to 34)

Report Description Formula & Interpretation Example
21 One-Way ANOVA F = MSB / MSW

Interpretation: A significant F-value means there is a difference among group means. 		Used to compare three or more independent groups.

Example: Comparing student performance across three different teaching methods.
22 Repeated Measures ANOVA F = variance between conditions / variance within subjects

Interpretation: A significant result means there is a difference over time or conditions. 		Used when the same group is measured multiple times.

Example: Measuring student performance before, during, and after an intervention.
23 Two-Way ANOVA F = effect variance / error variance

Interpretation: Determines main effects and interaction effects between two variables. 		Used to analyze two independent variables.

Example: Studying effects of teaching method and gender on performance.
24 Factorial ANOVA F = group variance / error variance

Interpretation: Shows interaction effects between multiple factors. 		Used when studying more than one factor at a time.

Example: Analyzing study habits, gender, and school type together.
33 Tukey HSD Test q = (mean difference) / standard error

Interpretation: Identifies which specific groups differ after ANOVA. 		Used after ANOVA to compare all group pairs.

Example: Finding which teaching method groups are significantly different.
34 Bonferroni Test α adjusted = α / number of comparisons

Interpretation: Controls Type I error when making multiple comparisons. 		Used to reduce error in multiple testing.

Example: Comparing multiple treatment groups while controlling error rate.

Module 6 - Chi-Square and Categorical Data Analysis

Chi-Square tests are used to analyze categorical data to determine whether there is a significant relationship between variables or whether observed frequencies differ from expected frequencies.

These tests are widely used in surveys, behavioral studies, and social science research where data are expressed in categories such as gender, preferences, or responses.

Report Description Formula & Interpretation Example
22 Chi-Square Test of Independence χ2 = Σ (O - E)2 / E

Interpretation: A significant result means two categorical variables are related. 		Used to test relationship between two categorical variables.

Example: Relationship between gender and preferred learning style.
23 Chi-Square Goodness of Fit χ2 = Σ (O - E)2 / E

Interpretation: A significant result means observed data differ from expected distribution. 		Used to compare observed frequencies with expected values.

Example: Checking if student grade distribution follows expected proportions.

Interpretation: Measures strength of association for two binary variables. 		Used for 2x2 categorical tables.

Example: Relationship between pass/fail and attendance status.
32 Cramér’s V V = sqrt(χ2 / [n(k - 1)])

Interpretation: Measures strength of association for larger categorical tables. 		Used for multi-category relationships.

Example: Relationship between program type and student satisfaction level.

Interpretation: Used when sample size is small and Chi-square assumptions are not met. 		Used for small sample categorical data.

64 Somers’ D D = (C - D) / (C + D + ties)

Interpretation: Measures directional association between ordinal variables. 		Used for ranked or ordinal categorical data.

Module 7 - Tests of Normality and Distribution Shape

Tests of normality are used to determine whether a dataset follows a normal distribution. These tests are important because many statistical methods assume that data are normally distributed.

This module also includes measures of distribution shape such as skewness and kurtosis, which describe how data are spread and shaped.

25 Shapiro-Wilk Test W test statistic

Interpretation: If p > 0.05, data are normally distributed; if p < 0.05, data are not normal. 		Used to test normality for small to medium sample sizes.

Example: Checking if student test scores follow a normal distribution.
35 Kolmogorov-Smirnov Test D = maximum difference between observed and expected distribution

Interpretation: If p > 0.05, data follow a normal distribution. 		Used to compare sample distribution with a normal distribution.

Example: Testing if employee performance ratings are normally distributed.

Module 8 - Measures of Association

Measures of association are statistical tools used to determine the strength and direction of relationships between variables. These are commonly used when analyzing categorical or ordinal data.

This module includes correlation-based measures, ordinal association tests, and predictive association statistics.


Overview of Reports (26 to 43 and 60 to 63)

Report Description Formula & Interpretation Example
26 Spearman Rank Correlation rs = 1 - (6Σd2 / n(n2 - 1))

Interpretation: Values close to +1 or -1 indicate strong monotonic relationships. 		Used for ranked or ordinal data.

Example: Relationship between class rank and student performance.
27

Kendall’s Tau

Report link

 τ = (C - D) / [n(n-1)/2]

Interpretation: Measures agreement between rankings. 		Used to compare ranking agreement.

28 Pearson Correlation (r) r = [nΣXY - (ΣX)(ΣY)] / sqrt([nΣX2 - (ΣX)2] [nΣY2 - (ΣY)2])

Interpretation: Values close to +1 or -1 indicate strong linear relationship. 		Used for continuous variables.

Example: Relationship between study hours and exam scores.
29 Point-Biserial Correlation r = (M1 - M0) / s × sqrt(pq)

Interpretation: Measures relationship between a binary and continuous variable. 		Used for yes/no or pass/fail data.

Module 9 - Post Hoc and Robust Tests

Post hoc and robust tests are used after ANOVA to determine exactly which group means are significantly different from each other.

These tests are applied when ANOVA results are significant, helping researchers identify specific group differences while controlling error rates.


Overview of Reports (45, 47, 48, 50 and 52 to 58)

Report Description Formula & Interpretation Example
45 Tukey Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) q = (Mean Difference) / Standard Error

Interpretation: Identifies which specific group means are significantly different. 		Used after one-way ANOVA for multiple comparisons.

Example: Determining which teaching methods differ in student performance.
47 Bonferroni Correction α adjusted = α / number of comparisons

Interpretation: Reduces Type I error when conducting multiple tests. 		Used to control error rate in multiple comparisons.

Example: Comparing several instructional strategies while avoiding false positives.
48 Scheffé Test F = (group variance / error variance)

Interpretation: Flexible test for all possible group comparisons. 		Used for complex or unplanned comparisons.

Example: Comparing performance across multiple school programs.
50 Dunnett Test t = (mean difference) / standard error

Interpretation: Compares multiple groups against a single control group. 		Used when comparing treatments to a control group.

Example: Comparing new teaching methods against a standard method.
52 Games-Howell Test t = mean difference / sqrt(variance components)

Interpretation: Used when variances are unequal and sample sizes differ. 		Used when ANOVA assumptions are violated.

Example: Comparing student performance across unequal group sizes.
53 Least Significant Difference (LSD) LSD = t critical × standard error

Interpretation: Detects pairwise differences between group means. 		Used for simple pairwise comparisons.

Example: Comparing two teaching methods after ANOVA.
54 Ryan-Einot-Gabriel-Welsch (REGWQ) Stepwise comparison procedure

Interpretation: Controls error rate while comparing multiple groups. 		Used for ordered multiple comparisons.

Example: Ranking school performance across districts.
55 Hochberg Test Adjusted p-value method

Interpretation: Controls family-wise error rate in multiple testing. 		Used for multiple hypothesis testing.

Example: Evaluating several educational interventions.
56 Holm-Bonferroni Method Stepwise α adjustment

Interpretation: Stronger version of Bonferroni correction. 		Used to reduce false positives in multiple comparisons.

Example: Comparing multiple curriculum models.
57 Welch ANOVA Post Hoc t = difference / standard error (unequal variances)

Interpretation: Used when homogeneity of variance is violated. 		Used for unequal variance group comparisons.

Example: Comparing student scores across different schools with unequal variance.
58 Robust ANOVA Methods Trimmed mean / variance adjustment

Interpretation: Provides reliable results even with outliers. 		Used when data contain outliers or non-normal distribution.

Example: Evaluating student performance with extreme score values.

Module 10 - Multivariate Statistical Analysis

Multivariate statistical analysis involves the examination of more than two variables simultaneously to understand complex relationships, patterns, and structures in data.

These methods are commonly used in advanced research to explain multiple outcomes, reduce data complexity, and identify underlying factors.


Overview of Reports (59 and 65 to 68)

Report Description Formula & Interpretation Example
59 Multiple Linear Regression Y = a + b1X1 + b2X2 + ... + bnXn

Interpretation: Shows how multiple predictors influence a single outcome variable. 		Used to predict outcomes using several variables.

Example: Predicting student performance based on study time, attendance, and motivation.
65 Factor Analysis X = ΛF + ε

Interpretation: Identifies underlying factors that explain relationships among variables. 		Used to reduce large datasets into key factors.

Example: Identifying key factors influencing student satisfaction.
66 Principal Component Analysis (PCA) Z = AX

Interpretation: Reduces data dimensions while preserving variance. 		Used for data reduction and pattern recognition.

Example: Reducing survey variables into main components for analysis.
67 MANOVA (Multivariate ANOVA) Wilks’ Lambda / Pillai’s Trace

Interpretation: Tests differences across groups for multiple dependent variables. 		Used when comparing group differences across several outcomes.

Example: Comparing teaching methods on both test scores and motivation levels.
68 Canonical Correlation Analysis Rc = correlation between variable sets

Interpretation: Measures relationship between two sets of multiple variables. 		Used to study relationships between two variable groups.

Example: Relationship between student characteristics and academic outcomes.
